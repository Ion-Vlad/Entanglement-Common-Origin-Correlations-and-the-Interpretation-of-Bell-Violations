# Entanglement-Common-Origin-Correlations-and-the-Interpretation-of-Bell-Violations
An alternative interpretation of quantum entanglement consistent with Bell's theorem.

## Overview

This repository contains the manuscript:

**Entanglement v1.0.pdf**

The paper presents an interpretive proposal regarding quantum entanglement and Bell correlations. It does **not** dispute Bell's theorem or the experimental Bell-test results. Instead, it explores whether the standard quantum correlation function may include an additional angular dependence beyond the minimal correlation associated with the common physical state established when an entangled pair is created.

The work is intended as a contribution to the discussion on the foundations and interpretation of quantum mechanics.

---

## Main Idea

The central hypothesis is that:

- The complete physical state of an entangled pair is established during the particle creation (or splitting) process.
- Subsequent measurements do not create the correlation but reveal properties associated with this pre-existing shared state.
- The standard quantum correlation function

E_quantum(θ) = -cos(θ)

may be interpreted as containing a supplementary angular contribution relative to a proposed local common-source correlation

E_local(θ).

The relationship is expressed as

E_quantum(θ) = E_local(θ) + F(θ),

where

F(θ) = E_quantum(θ) - E_local(θ)

is interpreted as a supplementary angular term.

---

## Purpose

The objective of this work is **not** to replace quantum mechanics or Bell's theorem.

Instead, it investigates whether:

- local common-source descriptions and
- standard quantum correlation functions

may represent complementary aspects of the same physical process.

## Status

This work should be regarded as a conceptual and interpretive proposal.

It does **not** claim experimental falsification of Bell's theorem or quantum mechanics.

Future work includes:

- deriving the proposed local correlation function from more fundamental physical assumptions,
- comparing the model quantitatively with Bell-test experiments,
- investigating possible experimental consequences.
